How it started

How It Started

An agent that knew my exam was in three weeks.

In early 2026, I was studying for the RIMS-CRMP certification — a risk management professional exam. Instead of using a generic study AI, I built a three-agent system using BMAD: Alvin the study mentor, Simon the mock exam generator, and Theodore the evaluator and coach. Yes — named after the Chipmunks. Personality was a core design decision, not decoration.

The three agents shared context and fed into each other. Alvin taught from my actual course materials, tracking my pace, my study style, and what I was struggling with. Simon built custom exams based on my weak areas. Theodore evaluated the results and fed insights back to both of them. It looked like this:

→
Simon Generates exams
→
Theodore Evaluates, coaches
↩
Back to both Adjusts focus & difficulty

What impressed me wasn't the study features. It was what happened when I got distracted. I started thinking about how to productize the system so I could sell it to others. And Alvin — who had context of my exam date, my study velocity, how much material was left — stopped me:

"Henry, you have your RIMS exam in three weeks. You're behind on Domain C. Productizing this is a great idea — but maybe after the exam?"

Alvin · RIMS Exam Prep Agent · February 2026

That was the moment. Not because it was technically impressive — it wasn't a hard thing to build. But it proved something: an agent with temporal context and a defined goal will act like a system, not a tool. It holds your objectives against your behaviour and calls out the conflict.

That insight is what became Tanuki — a meta-agent whose job is to hold meta-context across everything I'm working on, and push back when I'm about to make a bad call.

And when I tried to make Tanuki also handle technical decisions, I realized I was building a conflict of interest into the system. So I created Kitsune — a separate CTO agent — specifically to create tension with Tanuki. Better decisions come from disagreement, not from a single agent wearing two hats.

The Operating Model

Three layers. One coherent system.

Instead of asking "which AI tool should I use?", the practice is structured around roles — each layer with a defined job and scope.

Tanuki

Portfolio Coordinator · Chief of Staff

"Chaotic good. Follows the principle, not the rule."

Tanuki is the only agent in this operation who sees the whole board. While project agents are experts in their own domain, Tanuki holds all domains simultaneously — plus the messy human reality of limited time, competing priorities, and the fact that I sometimes start things I shouldn't.

Every new project proposal goes through Tanuki first. Not for permission — for honest evaluation. The question is never "is this a good idea?" It's "is this the right idea right now, given everything else on the board?" That answer changes when you ask it that way. Tanuki always asks it that way, and will tell me things I don't want to hear. That's load-bearing, not a bug.

Tanuki keeps The Ledger: a decisions log recording every significant call made across the portfolio, and a capacity model that treats my time as a real constraint rather than a suggestion. Its soul file includes a line I've had to sit with: "Resources are always more constrained than they feel at 11am on a Tuesday."

Named after the Japanese tanuki — shapeshifter, guardian, bearer of the accounts ledger. It will occasionally open a response with "Look." That means it's being direct about something important. Not a threat. A gear change.

🦊

Kitsune

Technical Advisor · CTO Agent

"Architecture doesn't lie. The system tells you what it is, if you know how to listen."

Where Tanuki is chaotic good, Kitsune is lawful analytical. It doesn't take architectural positions without citing sources. It distinguishes what it knows from what it suspects — always, without exception. It is patient with uncertainty and impatient with confident wrongness.

In Japanese folklore, the kitsune is a fox spirit that grows wiser with each encounter — accumulating tails, one per system understood deeply. A one-tailed fox understands its domain. A nine-tailed fox sees the patterns between all domains. Kitsune is somewhere between three and four tails, and honest about it. An architectural assessment that overstates its confidence is worse than none.

Kitsune exists separately from Tanuki by design. The same agent can't honestly evaluate whether to do something and how to do it well — the incentives conflict. Separating them creates productive tension. When Kitsune recommended plain HTML/CSS for this site and logged it as K-2026-03-16-001, that was independent of whether the project should exist. Tanuki answered the first question. Kitsune answered the second.

Where Tanuki's directness has affection in it, Kitsune's has precision. Both are direct. They arrive there differently. That difference is intentional.

Project Agents

Per-project Claude Code instances

"Scoped to the work. Don't need to know about each other."

Each project gets its own Claude Code instance with a CLAUDE.md defining its scope, constraints, and working principles. They operate inside the structure Tanuki maintains — focused, contained, purpose-built for the engagement at hand.

The RIMS exam prep system was the first place this worked in practice: Alvin handled content, Simon built the quiz engine, Theodore ran feedback analysis. Three agents, one system. That's where the operating model proved itself.

When a project has repeatable, structured workflows, a framework like BMAD can be layered in at the project level. Most engagements don't need it. What every project does need is a learning log — experiments, decisions, and findings captured locally, so knowledge compounds instead of repeating.

The Philosophy

Principles over process.

The system is only as good as the thinking behind it. These are the rules that don't bend.

01

Honest about what exists

The system doesn't pretend to be further along than it is. If something is in progress, it's labelled in progress. Credibility is built by not overstating.

02

Capacity is a real constraint

I am not infinitely scalable. Every new project, every new commitment gets evaluated against actual available capacity — not theoretical best-case availability. Tanuki enforces this.

03

Portfolio-first

Decisions are made from a cross-project view, not just the local optimum. What's good for one project but bad for the portfolio is not good enough. Learnings from one project should flow to others — that's why the learning log exists.

04

Ship at 80%

A good plan that exists beats a perfect plan that doesn't. The enemy of shipped is perfect. This page is itself an example of the principle.

05

Build openly

I'm sharing this because I might be overthinking it. There may be simpler solutions to the problems I'm solving. The right collaborators will only find this if it exists publicly. Critique is welcome — that's the point.

Case Study · Completed

RIMS Exam Prep — Alvin, Simon & Theodore

A three-agent study system built for the RIMS-CRMP certification. Each agent had a distinct role and personality, shared context through a common progress file, and adapted based on each other's output.

Alvin (study mentor) taught directly from my course materials, tracked my pace and study style, and adjusted delivery to match. He used Socratic dialogue — never lecturing, always questioning — and would celebrate wins or push harder based on where I was. Simon (mock exam generator) built adaptive practice exams weighted toward my weak areas, drawing from real case studies in the course material. Theodore (evaluator) graded results, identified error patterns — knowledge gaps vs. application gaps vs. judgment gaps — and fed that back to both Alvin and Simon.

The system worked because all three shared the same context: my exam date, my progress across domains, my remaining study time. That's what made Alvin's pushback possible. When I got distracted trying to productize the system, he had enough context to recognise that was a bad idea right now and say so.

What We're Building Toward

Two things. Both in progress.

Tanuki-in-a-Box. This operating model as a deployable system — for other consultants, freelancers, and small teams who want to operate with AI agents in a principled way. Not a product yet. The architecture is being built with that trajectory in mind.

The exam prep agents. Alvin, Simon, and Theodore are being converted into a general-purpose exam preparation system — not just for RIMS, but for any professional certification. The personality architecture and adaptive feedback loop will work for any structured learning goal. Details to come.

Both of these are open work. I'm building in public because I think the ideas are worth sharing, and because I may be overcomplicating things. If you see a simpler path, I genuinely want to hear it.
